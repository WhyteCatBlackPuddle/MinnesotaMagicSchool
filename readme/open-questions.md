# Open Questions

These are the next worldbuilding areas to define for Boundary Waters Academy. They are not blockers, but they are useful launch points for future sessions.

## Highest priority

- Define the lodge system: number of lodges, names, buildings, reputations, traditions, mentors, student leaders, rivalries, and how students are assigned.
- Rename the lake. Mirror Lake is a placeholder and should be replaced with a name that feels specific to this setting.
- Define what happened in the past between magical and non-magical society: the unjust forced separation, the forgetting, and the marks left on cities.
- Define the coming challenge that the school is quietly preparing magical society to face.

## School and campus

- Define the school's unofficial mission.
- Decide whether lodges fully replace houses and how they create school spirit without sorting students by personality or affinity.
- Name and describe the lodges, including unusual forms like a houseboat lodge, cellar lodge, tree dorm, and magically expanded cabins.
- Define school traditions: songs, poems, ceremonies, holidays, Winter Games, Spring Games, inter-school competitions, and the first winter survival ritual.
- Define the lower levels of the library and why students are forbidden from entering them.
- Define the village attached to the academy: coffee shop, library, post office, clothes boutique, butcher shop, bait shop, boat marina, and other practical magical businesses.
- Define village assignments and how students rotate through practical work.
- Define the school’s signature art form.

## Magic and ethics

- Define the major affinity categories, if any, while preserving that affinity is not destiny or curriculum.
- Define magical ethics rules, especially around coercion, consent, memory, identity, secrecy, and using magic on other people.
- Define common student safety rules beyond cold weather and supervised magic.
- Define what forms of magic are stigmatized, feared, or prestigious.
- Define how wands and other implements are chosen, inherited, made, supplied, or culturally varied.
- Define more magical implements beyond wands, books, orbs, gems, and necklaces.

## Wider magical society

- Define patron families: who they are, what they fund, what they expect, and why they are respected for better or worse.
- Define major magical urban centers in Minnesota: Minneapolis, St. Paul, Duluth, Rochester, Brainerd, and others.
- Define other magical schools in Minnesota and why some are considered more prestigious than Boundary Waters.
- Define magical government factions: old families, whitewashed gangs, guilds, wisdom councils, bloated government organizations, military groups, and commercial powers.
- Define practical magical careers and how Boundary Waters prepares students for them.
- Define how magical society uses or rejects technology.

## Faculty and staff

- Flesh out the Headmistress as the person who truly keeps the school running.
- Flesh out the military member who knows the most about the school’s true purpose and conflicts with the Headmistress.
- Flesh out the wilderness guide, who scares students most and is not trusted.
- Flesh out the Hearthmistress and Hearthmaster, the comforting married couple tied to keeping the buildings in good order.
- Define underestimated staff roles: postmaster, boatmaster, cooks, laundress/textile keeper, groundskeeper, infirmary staff, library assistants, shopkeepers, and maintenance workers.
- Decide whether any faculty or staff are non-human, partly human, or changed by their role.

## Students and relationships

- Revisit existing students through the new canon.
- For each student, define their affinity, how it first appeared, what they can do accidentally, what they misunderstand, and what danger or tension it creates.
- Assign students to lodges once the lodge system exists.
- Add social relationships: friends, rivals, lodge-mates, mentors, crushes, family ties, patron obligations, study partners, and groups that get in trouble together.
- Identify late bloomers and how their social status affects them.
- Make sure students feel like beginners with affinities, not heroes with powers.

## Tone boundaries to revisit

- Decide which real-world harms should be avoided entirely or only handled indirectly.
- Decide how dark bullying, curses, possession, memory magic, family trauma, and forced training can get before they stop fitting the project.
- Decide how the school can be morally wrong while remaining a place worth loving and preserving.
- Decide how religious themes appear in the world.
- Decide whether the story is firmly set in 2026 or should shift to 2006.

## App and website ideas

- Turn the World tab into a real content system instead of hardcoded summaries.
- Add a database table for world notes with category, title, summary, full text, status, and sort order.
- Add an Open Questions section to the website World tab.
- Add lodges as a data entity once the lodge system is defined.
- Add relationships as a data entity once student connections are mapped.
