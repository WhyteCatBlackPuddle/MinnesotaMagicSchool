# Boundary Waters Academy Institution

Boundary Waters Academy is a warm, rigorous, and dangerous boarding academy in the north woods of Minnesota. It is prestigious, rustic, outlandish, loved, scrappy, and known for excellent graduates. The school is a place of comfort and challenge where students often find themselves during their time there.

## Founding and origin

Boundary Waters Academy was founded around the time of the establishment of Minnesota as a European colony in the New World.

The founder was a full blood magical human: a man obsessed with the study of the magical flora and fauna of Minnesota. He was warm, loving, kind, full of laughter, practical, powerful, and a huge presence in other ways. People speak of him with kindness in their hearts. He was a renaissance man, and the colony he founded was almost a cult of personality as much as it was a research colony.

The academy began as an academic study project. It was founded by a coalition of magicians focused on fascinating new mythical herbology, animal study, and a powerful source of magic in the area that was shaping the region. Over time, the project drew in more people who saw the value of what was being done.

The school was not originally founded as a school. It was meant to be a place people could come to recover from being out in the field studying. The sauna is magical, and the goose down beds were known to restore both body and soul.

There was no crisis, war, magical disaster, discovery, or prophecy that led to its creation. It began with the optimism of colonists who had a vision of a new future in the New World.

The founders are remembered with accuracy because they left written documents about the founding of the school. Some of those documents are well known, but others are hidden from the students and some of the staff.

The school evolved over time into a place where young students can learn while practically helping with the study of magic in Minnesota.

## Purpose and mission

Boundary Waters Academy's purpose is to educate, train, and grow the next generation of magic leaders of the Midwest.

The school serves many of the same purposes as a military school, but with much more warmth and humanity. It exists because people need it to. It is a vital organ of the function of the magical society of Minnesota.

The school believes magical education is for the furtherance and protection of magic-kind.

The school tries to produce practical magic users who can contribute to the betterment of themselves and others. A successful graduate understands the magic world well and can grow in it.

A successful graduate must pass a set of aptitudes and tests. There is an underground military component to this, similar to how some countries give students weapons training in high school. A successful graduate has some aptitude in the magic world, not simply being able to cast. This aptitude might show through magical animal care, magical geography navigation, or other forms of practical magical competence.

The school considers it a failed education if a student cannot perform magic, cannot understand magic, or refuses to come under discipline with their use of magic.

The school values tradition, survival skills, independence, and curiosity.

The school would never openly admit that it exists to protect magical society as a whole and prepare magical society to protect itself from a coming challenge.

## Why this location matters

The school is located in the north woods of Minnesota because a site of magical importance was found there. Magical settlers were drawn to the area because of a distinct magical presence.

The lake near the school needs a better final name. It is located by the lake for practical reasons, including that it is spring fed and drinkable, but it is also a magic place. The lake is only somewhat magical, though there may be more yet to be discovered about it. It is more like a strategic military decision for a place in the woods.

The land is naturally magical. The remote location protects students from the world and protects the world from students.

The school has an obligation to protect the lake, forest, creatures, spirits, and hidden places nearby because of old promises.

Other magical places and schools exist in Minnesota, and Boundary Waters is not even the most prestigious one. There are magical schools in more urban places. Boundary Waters has a good reputation, but it has been reputed to be in decline. That reputation is not accurate. Other magical communities have a prejudice about the school that is not accurate.

## Size and structure

Boundary Waters Academy has about 300 students.

There are 8 years of schooling, with roughly 35 students per year. Students generally attend from around age 12 to 19 or 20.

Most students start at a standard age, but some can enter late. Late bloomers have a complicated relationship with the school and are usually socially outcast.

Everyone boards. Most faculty live on campus year-round, and some do not go home in the summer. Some faculty also have military, police, government, or commercial lives outside of the school.

There are staff who are not teachers, and they help keep the school running.

The adult-to-student ratio is small enough that the kids are allowed a little freedom because they cannot always be managed.

### Lodges

Students live in cabins or lodges rather than ordinary dorms. The lodges are warm, vulnerable, practical, and central to school life.

The school does not need Hogwarts-style houses, but lodges can serve some of the same social functions: belonging, spirit, competition, responsibility, and tradition. Students should not be grouped by affinity as if affinity were a fixed destiny.

Some lodges may be larger inside than outside and can be adjusted for size by a faculty enchanter. The campus may include varied lodge forms, including a traditional cabin, a houseboat lodge, a cellar lodge with a giant hearth and enchanted roof, and a large tree dorm.

Students have faculty mentors. There are also student leaders, cabin captains, apprentices, and older-student guides.

## Admissions and magical affinity

Students are sourced by the magic community and are mostly pulled from networks of known people. Patron families are financially involved with the school and help fund attendance.

A council of adults who run the school decides who should attend. All students are invited only, but parents can solicit the leaders of the school.

Magical families are expected to educate their children, but where they send them depends on family resources.

Children from non-magical families are discovered through social networks, testing, ancestry study, and other forms of magical investigation.

If parents refuse magical education, the child remains untrained. They retain some magical prowess, but it calms down after the puberty of magic.

Attendance may be required by magical law. A student refusing to attend is controversial and should create story tension. There should be some room for family choice, school invitation, legal pressure, and difficult edge cases.

There are costs involved, but attendance is mostly funded by patron families. Each student also has their own resources they can use to support attendance.

Students may be admitted because they are dangerous, cursed, gifted, politically important, or in need of protection.

There is no standard admissions test. The school tests affinity only in some cases, especially if a child is magic-born but not showing signs of magic. In cases involving non-magical families, there is testing and ancestry study.

A student with no obvious affinity is not excluded from attendance.

Some affinities are stigmatized or feared. Some affinities are considered prestigious.

## Curriculum and training

The purpose of first-year education is introduction to the magical world and getting all students on the same page in terms of knowledge and beginning practical skills.

By the end of first year, every student should learn wand casting, basic runes, simple warding, use of basic magical implements, and basic magical creature understanding.

Required subjects include:

- Enchantments
- Warding and Protection
- Casting and Offensive Protection
- Rune Carving
- Magical Implements Training
- Field Navigation and Survival
- Magical Creature Training
- Magic Botany and Cultivation
- Magic History and Culture

Village assignments are part of the school experience. The village should include places like a magic coffee shop, library, post office, magic clothes boutique, butcher shop, bait shop, and boat marina.

Students use wands and magical implements right away, after a lot of explanation about how dangerous such things are.

Wands are standard and personal. Sometimes they are handed down, sometimes they are custom made, and sometimes the school supplies them. Wands are the standard focus, but they are not the only cultural way students use magical focus.

Other implements include books, orbs, gems, necklaces, and other magical foci. Boundary Waters may also use practical local implements such as carved tokens, rune knives, lanterns, compasses, weather glass, textiles, and field gear.

Runes are taught very slowly and carefully.

Potions are scientific, mystical, culinary, medicinal, dangerous, and important. The potions and botany of the school are very important to its success.

Students receive some ordinary education, including practical math, writing, science, history, and languages. Magical students still need non-magical academics.

Students specialize after their 4th year. They may choose tracks, apprenticeships, electives, and concentrations. Older students are trained for careers and are sometimes apprenticed, interned, or pre-selected by magic companies or families for specific purposes.

Exams can be written, practical, oral, magical, field-based, dangerous, or mundane. Some are done in classrooms. Some are done outdoors so fires can be put out or students can be recovered safely. There can be a dungeoneering element.

Graduation requires a series of tests, practical exams, and aptitude proof judged by ex-students or members of the military or government.

## School culture and personality

Boundary Waters is more rigorous than many magical schools. It is cold, outdoorsy, serious, and sometimes fun. It is known as a tight-knit community brought together by mutual survival and caretaking.

It is prestigious, rustic, outlandish, loved, scrappy, and known for excellent graduates.

Outsiders stereotype Boundary Waters students as backwater, tick covered, and stinky. They also respect them for their power.

Students eventually become proud of their connection to the people who came before them.

The cabins and campus have worn over the years and appear to be in some obvious decline.

The school rewards ingenuity, prowess, discipline, passion, teamwork, care, intuition, growth, and strength.

The school punishes laziness, cowardice, bullying, lack of discipline, and not taking care of a fellow student.

The school is warm, nurturing, dangerous, traditional, a little chaotic, and often unsupervised.

Students love the school, fear it, and resent it. Some say the school has a presence in their world on its own.

Important traditions include a first-year inclusion ceremony, ritual of first winter survival course, rivalries between lodges, songs and poems, frequent quotes from the founder, Winter Games, Spring Games, and collegial competition with other magic schools. The school also celebrates normal real-world holidays because many students live in the real world as well.

Winter is enjoyable on campus. Outside campus, the extreme beauty and harshness of winter are present.

The lake is practical and has magical secrets that are not yet discovered.

The forest is alive, and the school mostly tries to keep it at bay.

Alumni become nostalgic when talking about old students, families, and ceremonies.

## Daily student life

Students sleep in beds in their cabins or lodges. Rooms may hold two students in normal beds or bunk beds. Some cabins are larger inside than outside and can be adjusted for size by a faculty enchanter.

The campus is sprawling, with a surprisingly large number of separated buildings for a place that gets so cold. Some of it is underground, which makes sense in a place with harsh winters. Much of the school is wood and stone buildings, and there is always an underground.

The main education happens in stone, brick, and wood buildings.

Students eat in a great hall with lots of warmth and togetherness. Students help make food along with some of the staff.

Breakfast includes normal food and magical food.

Students primarily travel by walking, boats, canoe, ski, skate, enchanted paths, and a shuttle like a horse-drawn carriage with an open roof that is magically enchanted to be warm.

Students wear uniforms, but they should be practical for Minnesota. Robes may exist, but cold-weather clothing, boots, coats, and field gear matter.

Phones, laptops, and internet are allowed, but they do not work.

Magical alternatives exist for communication and entertainment.

Mail is much more practical than owls. Magic culture is secretive and does not trust anyone but itself to deliver mail. The school uses practical and trusted magical delivery systems.

Students have chores and campus duties.

Weekends include campus duties, village jobs, date nights, trips to the lake beach, and overnights in the woods. Weekend life is pretty loose.

Student activities may include Camping Club, Botany Club, dueling, a mental game like chess, art, music, and school-specific magical arts. The school is known for a specific form of art that still needs to be defined.

Homesick students write letters, receive visits from parents, and sometimes go home during holidays.

Students break rules by being out past curfew or using magic they are not meant to use. There are lots of ways to be a rebellious teen in this world.

The coziest ordinary moment at the school is sitting in a cozy dorm in front of the magic flames.

The scariest ordinary moment at the school is getting lost by going only a little way into the forest.

## Rules, risks, and discipline

The school's most important rules are:

- Do not go into the forest.
- Do not use magic to attack one another.
- Stay out of the lower levels of the library.
- Do not go out into the middle of the lake.

Student safety rules include no magic use outside of classes until a certain year and keeping warm in winter.

The lake is safe along its shores, but the bottomless middle of the lake is to be avoided always. Fishing is permitted only on certain days. Students must respect the water creatures that inhabit the lake.

The forest should never be explored without a teacher. It has a mind of its own, and it cannot be trusted to take care of students. Many students' lives have been lost by not taking it seriously.

There are many kinds of creatures with different levels of self-awareness. Students should avoid creatures if they do not know what they are.

Spirits should not be trusted. They are rarely encountered, but are sometimes found in ancient places in the forest.

Old places may be used for dungeoneering challenges. Students know they risk their lives by doing so and must make sure they are well provisioned.

Every first-year eventually breaks the rule about not going into the forest.

The lake rule is never broken.

Students who use magic on each other lose wand privileges and are subject to physical labor without magic.

Students who cross a ward boundary receive a mark that lasts a while and are punished.

Punishments are always focused on restoring the student, but their form varies based on the faculty member punishing the student.

Discipline is handled by lodge heads, mentors, student courts, faculty councils, and headmistress decisions.

Common dangers that students train for include cold weather, getting lost, thin ice, unsafe water, magical exhaustion, miscast spells, wand and implement accidents, ward crossings, creature encounters, potion burns, rune misfires, forest disorientation, magical storms, accidental fires, dangerous plants, and field injuries.

Rare but terrifying dangers include dangerous creatures, magical weather events, unknown magical anomalies, magical sentient creatures, ancient spirits, the bottomless middle of the lake, forest rearrangement, ancient wards failing, and the hidden coming threat.

The school hides an oncoming danger from parents.

## Magical society

The broader magical world is practical, similar, and different. It is a parallel culture to the one it exists in and is influenced by. Magical people use both technology and magic, though most pure blood families do not use any technology. Magical society has its own social networks, economy, governmental organizations, commerce, and a range of financial strengths.

Patron families are common, known, and respected for better or for worse.

Major magical urban centers exist in Duluth, Rochester, Minneapolis, St. Paul, Brainerd, and basically anywhere there is a large Minnesota city.

Magical neighborhoods exist inside ordinary cities, but they are mostly hidden.

There is one magical government, but it is made from many united groups with competing interests. There are old families, whitewashed gangs, guilds, wisdom councils, and government organizations that have bloated past their original purpose.

Practical magical jobs exist in every field. If it is fishing, there is magical fishing. If it is clothes making, it can be done magically. There is a magical stock market, magical woodworking, and endless other magical trades.

What magical adults do all day depends on where they are in the social strata. Poor and middle-class people work. Upper classes have a lot more freedom. All find ways to enjoy themselves at the end of the day.

Magical hospitals, courts, shops, banks, universities, unions, newspapers, transit systems, prisons, and research institutes exist.

Magical families are mixed: wealthy, ordinary, hidden, struggling, diverse, and divided.

Magical society is secret from non-magical society because of past history.

Magical people sometimes use modern technology, but it is looked down on. Magic interferes with technology, especially electronics.

Urban magicians and rural or place-based magicians have social issues worth exploring.

Magical society expects Boundary Waters graduates to be competent. That is what matters practically, though there are many other social expectations that are not often spoken out loud.

## Relationship to non-magical society

Non-magical people know nothing about magic except folklore and other things meant to confuse them about it.

Magic is often right in front of non-magical people, but they dismiss it. It is not always hidden. There is a past that normal people ignore and have chosen to forget. Their cities bear the marks of what happened in the past.

Non-magical parents do not initially know what Boundary Waters Academy really is. When their children are tested, the parents are approached by the people who come to test the students. They are told only the bare minimum and are brought in slowly.

Students are allowed to tell non-magical friends.

When accidental magic happens in public, it is usually forgotten quickly, but not intentionally, because of what happened in the past.

Magical incidents are cleaned up through partnership between normal people and magical society.

There are laws about secrecy. Those laws are not always ethical and can be controversial.

Magical children from non-magical families feel caught between worlds.

Non-magical family members often visit the school.

The school communicates with non-magical parents through ordinary post and maybe through a working phone at the school that the staff protects.

There are tensions between magical and non-magical governments.

The separation between worlds is stable but fraying at the edges. What happened in the past was unjust and forced.

## Faculty and staff

Boundary Waters faculty are often outcasted, competent, former students, military members, resilient, and sometimes silly.

Faculty may be scholars, practitioners, protectors, eccentrics, retired professionals, and guardians. Teachers specialize through a mix of subject, affinity, career background, and magical tradition.

Faculty are mostly human, though non-human or partly human faculty may exist.

Faculty are allowed to have secrets, and often do.

Whether teachers are loyal to the school, the magical government, the land, old families, or themselves is the question of their lives.

Teachers are chosen and made obligated. Most come willingly.

There are staff positions students underestimate.

The headmistress actually keeps the school running.

The adult who scares students most is the wilderness guide. The wilderness guide is also the adult who is not trusted at all.

The adults who comfort students most are the Hearthmistress and Hearthmaster, a married couple tied to keeping the buildings in good order.

A military member knows the most about the school's true purpose. This military member has conflict with the headmistress because of the seriousness they feel due to what is coming.

A prized professor may be the adult most trusted by the wider magical world.

## Tone and story boundaries

The story can be human, but should not be unnecessarily macabre.

Death is possible. Student death can happen on-page. Faculty can die. Students can be villains.

Bullying should not become torture or murder. It should be a literary device to show the conflict in question.

Curses can cause permanent harm.

Magic can alter memory, identity, or consent, but those topics should be handled carefully.

Possession and body-horror elements are allowed.

Family abuse, neglect, and trauma can be part of student backstories.

Political oppression and forced secrecy can be central themes or background texture.

Students can be forced into dangerous training by adults.

The school building is feared, but it is also alive and sometimes influences or cares for the students in a kind way.

The school can be morally wrong, and those boundaries should be explored.

Romance is part of the student world, especially in later years. Butterflies, boyfriends, and girlfriends are part of the genre. Sex is off the table.

Religious themes are welcome.

Cosmic elements may enter the story, but the danger is mostly related to humans.

Real-world harms should be explored as they come up. Darker subjects should not be brought in if they are not worth bringing up.

Emotional themes of the students should always be treated with tenderness rather than spectacle.

When something frightening happens, warmth is restored by other characters caring, the hominess of the dorms, the comforting headmistress and staff, and warm things that welcome students back.

The project should leave readers or users with comfort, adventure, exploration, and wonder.

Boundary Waters should never become a clone of its inspirations, and as a place, it should never die out. It should always preserve being a place of comfort and learning where students often find themselves.
